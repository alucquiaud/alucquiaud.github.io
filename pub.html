Analytical derivations of critical tolerance thresholds in the Schelling model

A. Lucquiaud, J. Randon-Furling

(nov. 2021)
Conference on Complex Systems 2021, Lyon, France

Abstract
Schelling's is one of the oldest and most famous agent-based models in the social sciences. It reveals the emergence of macroscopic segregation in a system of agents belonging to two groups and interacting at a microscopic level on a 2D lattice. The system’s behaviour has been studied extensively through numerical simulations, leading to phase diagrams that show which stationary macrostate (mixed or segregated) is reached depending on the values of a handful of parameters. The most important one is the so-called “tolerance” T: an agent will move to another location if a fraction strictly larger than T of their neighbours belongs to the other group.

Consistent with Schelling’s counter-intuitive observations, a first phase transition – from low tolerance mixed equilibria to segregated macrostate – occurs around T=1/2 when vacant sites are scarce, or when unsatisfied agents are allowed to trade positions, in a Kawasaki-type exchange process. We derive this transition in a dynamical setup, using Markov chain aggregation techniques: to some extent, the system’s behaviour resembles that of an urn from which pairs of agents would be drawn and swapped. This analogy enables us to compute the transition matrix and invariant distribution, which we compare to the empirical study of attractor states.

Since Schelling’s seminal work, the existence of a second phase transition has also been confirmed at a value Tc of the tolerance with 1/2≤Tc≤3/4 – the exact value varies with the local neighbourhood size. We build upon previous works that focus on the formation of homogeneous clusters and use asymptotic results of percolation theory in infinite systems with infinitely large neighbourhoods. In a simpler, finite setup, mimicking nucleation models from statistical physics, we derive analytically the value of Tc from simple conditions on the stability of small homogeneous clusters, that act as nuclei in the transition toward segregation. For 1/2<T<Tc, the stationary state is one of separation between homogeneous clusters, while for T>Tc it is one of integration, as observed on the wealth of existing simulation results.

Markov chain aggregation in the Schelling model

A. Lucquiaud, J. Randon-Furling

(mai 2021)
French Regional Conference on Complex Systems 2021, Dijon, France

Abstract
Schelling's 1971 multi-agent model revealed the emergence of macroscopic segregation in a system of interacting agents even when individual preferences do not favour homogeneous neighbourhoods over mixed neighbourhoods. The behaviour of the origin Schelling model, and of its abundant variants in the literature, has been studied extensively through numerical simulations, leading to phase diagrams showing which stationary macrostate (mixed or segregated) is reached depending on the values of a handful of parameters. One of these parameters is the so-called "tolerance" T: the maximum level of heterogeneity for which an agent is still satisfied with her neighbourhood - above this level, she will move to another site in the system. Starting from Schelling's observations, phase transitions have been shown to occur in the simulations when T is increased from 0 through to 1.

We present here an analytical approach of the Schelling model, using Markov chain aggregation techniques. The initial space of microscopic states is reduced into a smaller state space by aggregating micro-states that lead to the same value for a macroscopic variable | in that case the interface density. We derive the transition matrix of the corresponding aggregated Markov chain, and we determine a critical value of T that characterizes the phase transition between segregated and mixed stationary states.

Urban segregation: a micro-mobility induced phenomenon displaying multiscalar complexity

A. Lucquiaud

(nov. 2019)
Human mobility: from big data to applications, La Havana, Cuba

Abstract
We present a multifocal approach to reveal spatial dissimilarities in real cities, from the most local scale to the metropolitan one. Any statistical variable (e.g. social housing rate, ethnic group proportions) can be measured at larger and larger scales, starting from any unit block, and yields a beam of sequences revealing the city's underlying spatial structure.

At the individual scale, Schelling's model of segregation is useful to understand the emergence of this structure. Although it has been actively studied in various fields from economics to statistical physics, its spatial nature has not been adressed analytically yet. We will present an analytical derivation using Markov chains to determine the dynamics and thermodynamics of such a system.
